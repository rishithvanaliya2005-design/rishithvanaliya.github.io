Research Internship · IIT Bombay

Multiphase Flow Modeling using Physics-Informed Neural Networks

Research internship at the CFD Lab, IIT Bombay under Prof. Atul Sharma focused on level-set PINN formulations for interface evolution and multiphase flow problems.

Overview Research Work Methods Gallery

Research Overview

This work was carried out during my research internship at the Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) Lab, IIT Bombay under the guidance of Prof. Atul Sharma. The research focused on extending Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) toward challenging multiphase flow problems involving moving interfaces, density discontinuities, and bubble dynamics.

Traditional computational fluid dynamics methods provide highly accurate solutions for multiphase systems, but often require fine meshes, interface reconstruction techniques, and significant computational resources. This work explored whether physics-informed learning approaches could reproduce interface evolution and fluid dynamics behavior while reducing dependence on expensive mesh-based formulations.

The research emphasized level-set-based PINN formulations, where governing physics and interface evolution equations were embedded directly into the neural network optimization process. The objective was not only to reproduce benchmark solutions, but also to investigate the stability and robustness of different differentiation strategies within PINNs.

CFD Lab · IIT Bombay

The CFD Lab at IIT Bombay works on a broad range of problems in computational fluid mechanics, multiphase flow physics, heat transfer, and high-fidelity numerical modeling. The lab combines classical numerical methods with modern computational approaches for solving challenging transport and interface dynamics problems.

During the internship, I worked under Prof. Atul Sharma, whose research spans multiphase flow modeling, interface-capturing methods, boiling and bubble dynamics, and advanced CFD methodologies. The internship provided exposure to both rigorous physical modeling and research-oriented scientific computing workflows.

Research Work

Passive Bubble Transport

One part of the work focused on passive bubble transport problems, where the objective was to accurately evolve interfaces under prescribed velocity fields using level-set formulations. These studies served as controlled benchmarks for evaluating the ability of PINNs to capture interface advection without introducing excessive numerical diffusion.

Different differentiation strategies were investigated to study their influence on interface sharpness, convergence stability, and long-time evolution behavior.

Static Bubble Dynamics

The research also investigated static bubble problems involving surface tension and curvature-driven effects. These cases are particularly sensitive to numerical instability because small curvature errors can generate non-physical pressure oscillations near the interface.

PINN-based formulations were evaluated for their ability to preserve interface smoothness and maintain physically consistent pressure distributions across the phase boundary.

Bubble Rise Simulations

A major component of the internship involved bubble rise problems, where buoyancy-driven interface motion introduces strong coupling between velocity, pressure, and interface evolution. These simulations are significantly more complex because the interface dynamically deforms while interacting with the surrounding fluid field.

The work investigated whether PINNs could reliably capture bubble deformation, rise velocity, and transient interface behavior without relying on conventional mesh-based interface tracking techniques.

Methods and Formulations

Level-Set PINN Framework

The interface was represented using a level-set formulation, where the signed distance function evolves according to governing transport equations. Physics constraints were embedded directly into the neural network loss function, enabling simultaneous learning of flow fields and interface dynamics.

Differentiation Strategies

A central focus of the internship was comparing different differentiation approaches used within PINNs:

  • Automatic Differentiation (AD) — standard PINN differentiation using exact computational graphs.
  • Numerical Differentiation (ND) — finite-difference-based derivative approximations integrated within the learning framework.
  • Coupled Automatic-Numerical (CAN) — hybrid differentiation strategy combining AD and ND to improve interface stability and gradient behavior.

These approaches were evaluated based on convergence behavior, stability, interface preservation, and accuracy across different multiphase benchmark problems.

Key Takeaways

  • Developed practical experience with level-set formulations for multiphase systems.
  • Investigated limitations of PINNs in highly nonlinear interface evolution problems.
  • Explored the tradeoff between physical consistency, stability, and computational cost in mesh-free solvers.
  • Worked extensively with scientific computing workflows, large-scale training pipelines, and CFD-oriented validation.
  • Gained exposure to research methodology, numerical benchmarking, and multiphase flow physics in an academic research environment.
